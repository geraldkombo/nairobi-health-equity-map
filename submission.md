# The Kenya Health Equity Map: Community Evidence for Health Justice

*Gerald Kombo — Independent Researcher and Developer, Kenya Health Equity Map*

## Sub-theme 2: Digital Health and Evidence Generation Through Community-Led Monitoring

---

## 1. The Problem: The Statistical Illusion of Progress

Kenya's health indicators are moving in the right direction. Under-5 mortality has fallen from 115 to 41 per 1,000 live births since 2003. Facility delivery rates have doubled. These are real achievements. But aggregate statistics, by their nature, create a statistical illusion that masks the communities left behind.

Behind every national average lies a county where the data tells a different story: a mother in Turkana who cannot reach a clinic, a household in Tana River for whom a hospital visit means choosing between transport and food, a community in Mandera where the nearest skilled birth attendant is hours away.

The national C-section rate of 17% conceals a stark divide: 34% among the wealthiest households versus near-zero access in the poorest ASAL communities. When data is only ever examined in aggregate, inequity becomes invisible by design.

Community-Led Monitoring was designed to bridge this gap. But CLM is only as powerful as the evidence communities can access and understand. Today, the data that could transform CLM from a process into an outcome is scattered across government portals, locked in technical reports written for donors rather than for the people most affected by health inequity.

The Kenya Health Equity Map closes this evidence gap by making existing public data visible, understandable, and actionable for the communities it describes.

---

## 2. What the Platform Is

A free, offline-first digital map that translates every Kenyan county's health infrastructure reality into plain language.

| Feature | What It Does | Why It Matters for CLM |
|---|---|---|
| **Priority Gap Score** | A simple 0–100 score combining travel time to clinic, poverty rate, and population per facility. Higher score = greater inequity. | Communities have a **measurable, trackable baseline**. Turkana scores 92. If resource allocation improves, the score drops. CLM impact becomes quantifiable for Global Fund reporting. |
| **County Brief (printable)** | A one-page brief for any county with PGS, component breakdown, indicator data, and source citations. | Establishes a **shared, objective foundation for CHMT discussions**. Every number has a source URL, providing government officers with a reliable planning tool. |
| **Side-by-Side Comparison** | Compare any two counties across all indicators. | A community in Elgeyo-Marakwet can compare directly with Nairobi. Regional coordinators rapidly evaluate multiple neighboring counties. |
| **Offline Mode** | Works without internet after first load. No login, no installation, no app store. | A health promoter in a remote ASAL village can **use the evidence on a basic smartphone** without data credit. Digital inclusion is the foundation. |

### The Priority Gap Score in Plain Language

The score combines three factors that define a community's real access to health. It is a verifiable paper calculation, not an algorithm:

| Factor | What It Measures | Evidence It Reveals |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical Access** | How many minutes to reach the nearest clinic? | In ASAL counties, travel exceeds 90 minutes. Distance is both a barrier to care and a quantifiable advocacy target. |
| **Economic Vulnerability** | What percentage of the population lives below the poverty line? | Tana River: 72.5% poverty. When a household cannot absorb out-of-pocket costs, the data provides a foundation for free maternal care policies. |
| **Population Pressure** | How many people depend on each facility? | Elgeyo-Marakwet: 454,000 people sharing 1 mapped facility. The ratio alone makes a clear case for infrastructure investment. |

**Result:** Nairobi scores 40 (lowest inequity, 618 facilities, 16.5% poverty). Turkana scores 92 (highest inequity, 42.6% hardcore poverty — the highest nationally). The 52-point gap maps directly onto the lived reality of a woman in Turkana who must choose between a 90-minute walk to a clinic and giving birth at home.

## 3. How Communities Are Using This Evidence

### 3.1 Validating Lived Experience with Quantified Data

A community-based organization in Turkana uses the map to document that the county's facility gap is a measurable reality. The PGS of 92, combined with a 47% home birth rate and a 42.6% hardcore poverty rate — encompassing over 745,000 individuals — provides a quantified, source-cited document for the County Health Management Team. The conversation elevates from subjective feedback to a structural review of quantified infrastructure gaps.

### 3.2 Tracking Whether Conditions Improve

In Mandera (50% home births, PGS 91), a women's peer network uses the county brief as a fixed baseline. They track the PGS over time to support the establishment of emergency transport funds and decentralized maternity waiting homes. If the travel time component drops from 90+ minutes to 60 minutes after a new clinic opens, the score moves, demonstrating a measurable outcome from collaborative planning.

### 3.3 Strengthening Budget Allocations

In Tana River, a community health committee used the verified 72.5% poverty rate and 48% home birth rate to build an evidence-based case for removing financial barriers at local dispensaries during county health planning forums and CHMT quarterly meetings. Tana River's Rural Access Index of just 11.35% underscores why structural poverty must be visible to budget planners.

### 3.4 Cross-County Peer Coordination

Regional peer networks spanning multiple counties use the comparison tool to identify which members face the greatest infrastructure deficits. Coordinators rapidly cycle through neighboring county pairs during planning sessions, building a comprehensive regional picture in minutes.

## 5. Strategic Alignment with Existing Investments

This platform strengthens existing digital health investments by adding a spatial evidence layer that complements current tools:

- **iMonitor:** Enables communities to report service quality. This map provides the spatial infrastructure context. Together they form a complete CLM evidence loop.
- **eCHIS & Community Health Promoters:** eCHIS answers "what did we deliver?" This map answers "where is the infrastructure gap?" It equips Kenya's 107,000+ CHPs with evidence to carry into CHMT meetings.
- **THRIVE Project:** The comparison tools can overlay infrastructure equity data onto THRIVE digital dashboards for precise planning.
- **Global Fund CSS Coordination:** Provides a measurable CLM outcome indicator, tracking accessibility gaps and resource allocation over time.

---

## 6. Data Justice by Design

Aggregate national averages render local crises invisible. Data disaggregation is a precondition for justice. Without county-level granularity, resource allocation defaults to the status quo.

The platform is transparent by construction. Every data point carries a direct source URL. There is no proprietary algorithm. It also collects zero user data. A health promoter operating in a marginalized area does not need to trust assurances about data safety because there is no data to collect.

---

## 7. Limitations: An Invitation for Community Action

The platform draws on 1,699 community-mapped health facilities from OpenStreetMap, representing approximately 10% of Kenya's estimated total. Elgeyo-Marakwet County illustrates this gap: the official Master Facility List records 129 facilities, yet OpenStreetMap reflects fewer than 20. This renders the majority digitally invisible for spatial planning.

This is not a flaw, but a feature of the platform's design philosophy: communities can contribute missing facility coordinates to OpenStreetMap, directly strengthening the evidence base that informs their own advocacy.

---

## 8. Sustainability: Built to Outlive Funding Cycles

The Kenya Health Equity Map requires zero ongoing hosting costs. There is no server, no database, no maintenance overhead. It is freely available for any community to use or adapt, and it functions independently of donor funding cycles — directly addressing the need for sustainable CLM tools.

---

## 10. Conclusion

The Kenya Health Equity Map demonstrates that digital health tools serve Community-Led Monitoring best when built as genuinely community-owned evidence platforms. By addressing digital exclusion through offline-first architecture, zero-cost access, and plain-language transparency, it puts spatial analysis into the hands of the peer-led networks on the frontlines of health equity work.
